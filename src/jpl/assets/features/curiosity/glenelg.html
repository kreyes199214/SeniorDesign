Continuing eastward, Curiosity spent over 200 sols in a region known as Glenelg. This area marks a junction of 3 different terrain types and provided Curiosity with the opportunity to examine bedrock that was being exposed as the wind eroded the overlying mudstone. At Glenelg, in a shallow depression known as Yellowknife Bay, Curiosity made use of its drill to obtain samples from mudstone sedimentary rock formations that were named John Klein and Cumberland. The trip to Glenelg was a significant detour for Curiosity on its journey to Mt. Sharp. But it was selected for investigation because previous observations from orbit by the Mars Reconnaissance Orbiter and other spacecraft indicated that water had played a major role in forming rock deposits here. The detour turned out to be worth it.
